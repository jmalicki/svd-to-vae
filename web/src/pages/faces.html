Squeezing a face through a few numbers

Source on GitHub

Time to connect the math to something in the real world. Up to now the SVD acted on toy matrices; here we point the same truncated SVD at a stack of face photos and ask a practical question: can a handful of numbers still look like this person?

From truncated SVD to a face

Faces are a good real-world test for two reasons. First, you already know what “still looks like this person” means — you do not need a formula to judge the result. Second, a photo is thousands of pixels, so if we can store someone as a short list of numbers instead of the whole image, that is genuinely useful.

  1. Start from what you already know. On the previous page we took a matrix, wrote it with an SVD, and then truncated it: keep only the top $k$ singular components, throw the rest away, and rebuild an approximation. That recipe never cared what the numbers in the matrix meant.

  2. Here the matrix is a stack of faces. Rough idea: line the photos up so the same spot in every picture is roughly the same feature (eye, mouth, …), stretch each lined-up picture into one long column, and put about 240 columns side by side. Call that matrix $X$. Every entry is a brightness at a corresponding place on a face — not a toy entry, and not a random pixel from an unaligned photo either. (How the lining-up works lives in the appendix.)

  3. What the SVD gives you. Same objects as always: shared directions for the whole stack (reusable “face patterns,” ordered by importance), and — for each face — a short list of coordinates saying how much of each pattern this person needs. Truncating to rank $k$ means keeping only the first $k$ of those coordinates and rebuilding a picture from just that list. The middle tile in the demo is that rebuild.

Scrub $k$ below. Large $k$ can match the left tile; small $k$ is the interesting squeeze — when does the rebuild stop looking like this person?

Appendix — representation, warp, and further reading

  • What $X$ actually is. After lining faces up (next item), each face is a $64\times 64$ grayscale image. Flatten it to a vector $x_j\in\mathbb{R}^{4096}$ and put the $N=240$ faces as columns of a matrix $X=\bigl[x_1\ \cdots\ x_N\bigr]\in\mathbb{R}^{4096\times 240}$. Subtract the mean column $\bar x$ so we work with centered data $\tilde X = X - \bar x\,\mathbf{1}^{\top}$. An SVD $\tilde X = U\,\mathrm{diag}(\sigma)\,V^{\top}$ gives orthonormal columns $u_1,u_2,\ldots$ of $U$ (shared appearance directions) and singular values $\sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\ge\cdots$. Face $j$’s coordinates in that basis are the inner products $c_{ij}=u_i^{\top}(x_j-\bar x)$ — the bars in the demo; the amber curve is the $\sigma$’s. Truncating to rank $k$ keeps only the first $k$ directions: $$\hat x_j = \bar x + \sum_{i=1}^{k} c_{ij}\,u_i,$$ i.e. the closest vector to $x_j$ in the affine subspace $\bar x + \mathrm{span}\{u_1,\ldots,u_k\}$. Left demo tile $=x_j$; middle $=\hat x_j$; right $=\bar x$ ($k=0$).
  • Why warp first. Raw photos put eyes and mouths in different places. If you flatten unaligned crops into vectors and take an SVD, early singular vectors spend their energy on “where is the feature?” rather than “what does it look like?” We mark the same landmarks on every face, Procrustes-align the landmark shapes, then piecewise-affine warp on a Delaunay mesh (delaunator) onto a shared mean shape.

    In linear-algebra language: each face is still a vector in $\mathbb{R}^{4096}$ after the warp — the ambient dimension does not change. What the warp changes is the meaning of the coordinates: entry $i$ of every $x_j$ is brightness at roughly the same place on the face. Without that, entry $i$ might be an eye in one column of $X$ and a cheek in another, so the columns are not comparable as vectors in a shared basis. The step that actually lowers dimension is truncated SVD: replace $x_j\in\mathbb{R}^{4096}$ by the $k$ coefficients $(c_{1j},\ldots,c_{kj})$, i.e. work in a $k$-dimensional subspace. (Landmark / shape variation is modeled separately in the same literature; this page’s $k$ is for the warped appearance vector.)
  • Data. IMM Face Database — 240 landmarked faces (40 subjects × 6). Cite: M. B. Stegmann, B. K. Ersbøll, and R. Larsen. FAME – a flexible appearance modelling environment. IEEE Trans. on Medical Imaging, 22(10):1319–1331, 2003.
  • Further reading. Line up faces, then factor the appearance matrix — same idea as classical statistical shape / appearance models. Next stops: Turk & Pentland, Eigenfaces (PCA on pixel vectors with no warp — the foil this page avoids); Cootes, Edwards & Taylor, Active Appearance Models (shape alignment + appearance together); and the IMM / FAME paper above for the landmarked data used here.
