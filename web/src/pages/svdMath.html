Singular value decomposition — and keeping only some stretches

Source on GitHub

The last page showed how reflections introduce zeros and how left and right orthogonal maps can thin a matrix. Here we name the finished factorization, sketch how libraries compute it, then keep only the largest stretches.

The factorization

Any real matrix $A$ can be written as two orthogonal maps and a list of nonnegative stretches — chapter 1’s rotate → stretch → rotate, now for any size:

$$A = U\,\mathrm{diag}(\sigma)\,V^{\top}$$

Columns of $U$ and $V$ are orthonormal ($U^{\top}U = I$, $V^{\top}V = I$). The numbers $\sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\ge\cdots\ge 0$ are the singular values — the stretch amounts. People call this the singular value decomposition (SVD).

How one computes it

The finished factors below come from a library. The idea behind them is the tool from the previous page: a Householder reflection that aims a whole vector onto a coordinate axis, introducing many zeros at once. Used from both sides, that is enough to reduce $A$ to a thin form whose stretches are almost the singular values.

  1. Left multiply clears a column. Take column 1 of $A$. Build a Householder $H$ so $H\,\mathrm{col}_1 = \bigl(\|\mathrm{col}_1\|,\,0,\ldots,0\bigr)^{\top}$ — same construction as aiming an arrow onto the axis. Replace $A$ by $HA$. Column 1 now has a single pivot with zeros underneath; the other columns move too because every column is multiplied by the same $H$.

  2. Right multiply clears a row. The same idea works on the right. Choose a Householder $Q$ acting on the columns (equivalently: aim a row of $A$ the way you aimed a column) and replace $A$ by $AQ$. You can zero entries in a chosen row past a chosen position, without changing lengths of outputs — only which inputs go where. The $2\times 2$ “left then right” demo on the previous page is exactly one left clear and one right clear.

  3. Alternate until $A$ is bidiagonal. Clear under the first pivot (left), then clear the first row past the adjacent entry (right), then under the second pivot, and so on. After a full pass you get a bidiagonal matrix — nonzeros only on the main diagonal and one neighboring band:

    $$\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{left}} \begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * \\ 0 & * & * & * \\ 0 & * & * & * \\ 0 & * & * & * \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{right}} \begin{bmatrix} * & * & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & * & * & * \\ 0 & * & * & * \\ 0 & * & * & * \end{bmatrix} \to \cdots \to \begin{bmatrix} * & * & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & * & * & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & * & * \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & * \end{bmatrix}$$

    You stop at this thin band on purpose. Trying to wipe the last off-diagonal with the same style of reflection in one shot tends to fill in zeros you already made. Better to leave a simple band and finish it with a second phase.

  4. Iterate on the band; record the maps. An iterative phase drives the remaining off-diagonal entries to zero. What is left on the diagonal (absolute values, sorted) are the singular values $\sigma_j$. Every left Householder you applied is accumulated into the orthogonal factor $U$; every right one into $V$. Undoing the bookkeeping gives $A = U\,\mathrm{diag}(\sigma)\,V^{\top}$.

  5. Libraries follow this outline (often called Golub–Kahan bidiagonalization, then iteration on the band). The heatmaps below show the finished $U$, $\sigma$, and $V$ — not every intermediate zero.

Keep only some of the stretches

Once you have many $\sigma$’s, you can keep only the largest $k$ and rebuild $\hat A_k = U_k\,\mathrm{diag}(\sigma_{1:k})\,V_k^{\top}$. That is a truncated singular value decomposition (truncated SVD). The discarded energy is exactly the sum of the dropped singular values squared:

$$\|A - \hat A_k\|_F^{2} = \sum_{j>k}\sigma_j^{2}$$

By the Eckart–Young–Mirsky theorem, no other rank-$k$ matrix beats $\hat A_k$ in Frobenius error. Scrub $k$ below: watch the rebuild, the residual $A-\hat A_k$, and that identity.

Appendix

Frobenius norm

The Frobenius norm is the elementwise $\ell_2$ norm — square every entry, add them up, take a square root:

$$\|M\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j} M_{ij}^{2}},\qquad \|M\|_F^{2} = \sum_{i,j} M_{ij}^{2}.$$

Squared Frobenius error $\|A-\hat A\|_F^{2}$ is the sum of squared entrywise differences — the same notion as mean squared error when every entry counts equally.

Eckart–Young–Mirsky theorem

Among all rank-$k$ matrices $B$, the truncated reconstruction $\hat A_k$ uniquely minimizes $\|A - B\|_F$ (and therefore $\|A - B\|_F^{2}$).

$$\min_{\mathrm{rank}(B)\le k}\;\|A - B\|_F = \|A - U_k\,\mathrm{diag}(\sigma)\,V_k^{\top}\|_F$$

Bidiagonalization

The left/right Householder pass that produces a bidiagonal matrix is often called Golub–Kahan bidiagonalization. The follow-up iteration on that band (typically with plane rotations) finishes the singular values. This page shows the algebraic outline and the finished factors, not a step-by-step simulator of the iteration.

In short

The singular value decomposition writes $A = U\,\mathrm{diag}(\sigma)\,V^{\top}$. One computes it by clearing columns and rows with Householder reflections until $A$ is bidiagonal, then iterating on that band; $U$ and $V$ record the reflections. Truncating to the largest $k$ singular values gives the best rank-$k$ approximation in Frobenius norm, with error $\sum_{j>k}\sigma_j^{2}$.
